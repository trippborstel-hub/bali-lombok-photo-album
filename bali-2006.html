Ubud

Our home base in the heart of Bali

This place is exquisite... where to begin. Bali has 3 million people, and a culture that is about a thousand years old and is immersed in gratitude. Gratitude for the rice, for the rains and waters, for each other, for the animals, for being alive. It's such a stark difference with the Western world where time is money and tightly compressed. Here time is ceremony.

Culture & Ceremony

Gratitude woven into daily life

At nearly every entrance, and every shrine there is a daily offering made. These offerings are an art form in themselves. Palm leaves weaved into a small basket, the form of which depends on the time of year, and cycle of the moon. Inside of which a small offering is placed — perhaps a little rice, an orchid, some sweetmeats. You can't walk down the sidewalks of Ubud without stepping on one. Some are to the gods, others for good business, or perhaps wishing someone well. With such deep gratitude in their hearts, it's no wonder they are among the most friendly and present people on the planet.

Ayung River Walk

Hiking the river valley near Sayan

Yesterday we walked along the Ayung River, near Sayan outside of Ubud. It's a luscious river valley with rice terraces along the banks, and forests of palm trees lining the ridge, from which the volcanoes that the island was birthed can be seen. We found a young guide at the head of the trail, who was all too happy to escort us on our hike. Like so many people here on the island, the downturn in tourism has hit hard — he only has a few days of work at a restaurant, so in his off days he leads these hikes.

As almost every piece of land on Bali has been shaped by humans in some way, its meaning and purpose is not apparent to western eyes. The only source of fresh water in Bali is rain, which starts in the mountains and flows to the sea. In order to make sure that farmers at the beginning of a river don't take water at the expense of the farmer downstream, the Balinese have created organizations called sabaks. Every six months the sabaks meet in ceremony to decide how much water is available, how it should be distributed, and what crops each farmer should plant.

Art & Daily Life

Beauty is everywhere

The Balinese love their land, and see no separation between themselves and the land. There are gods of the rice, gods of the rivers, all of which must be kept happy so that they can continue to grow food, nourish their bodies, and live a healthy life. One of the results is that there is such care in how they work. Work is the manifestation of beauty rather than something to be done for the sake of achievement and self-glorification. Art is everywhere — even a modern petrol station is adorned with textiles, stone carvings of guardians, and offerings of flowers. They are a highly adaptable people, absorbing the modern world with a style and grace all their own.

The World in April 2006

  • Film & TV "The Sopranos" was in the middle of its final season. "Ice Age: The Meltdown" was #1 at the box office. "Lost" and "24" dominated TV ratings.

  • World News Italy elected Romano Prodi as Prime Minister. Nepal's King Gyanendra restored parliament after weeks of pro-democracy protests. Iran announced it had successfully enriched uranium.
  • Sports The 2006 FIFA World Cup in Germany was two months away, with anticipation building worldwide. Barry Bonds was chasing Babe Ruth's home run record with #714.
  • Indonesia Bali was still recovering from the 2005 bombings. Tourism had dropped significantly, deeply affecting the local economy — something Tripp and Syd witnessed firsthand through their guide's story.
